Exponential Distribution is Normal?

Giulio Mario Martena

6/3/2020

Overview

The purpose of this analysis is to verify the similarities between a normal an exponential distribution.

  • The exponential distribution is closely related to the Poisson distribution, and can be simulated in R with rexp(n,), where is the rate parameter.

The mean of the exponential distribution is 1/, and the standard deviation is also 1/.

In this study will be fixed to 0.2.

The distribution of averages across 40 exponential will be investigated in this study, across 1000 simulations.

Exponential distribution simulation

 <- 0.2 
n <- 40 # Number of distributions
sims <- 1000 # Number of simulations per distribution

simulation <- replicate(sims, rexp(n,)) #replicate returns a matrix
meanExp <- apply(simulation,2,mean)

Sample Mean versus Theoretical Mean

meanSample <- mean(meanExp)
meanSample
## [1] 4.941153
meanTheoretical <- 1 / 
meanTheoretical
## [1] 5

The sample mean is 4.94, while the theoretical is 5. The difference between the two is 0.06, which is quite similar. The distribution is plotted in plot 1.

  • Remark: the means are close to each other, and represent the center of the distribution.

The natural next step is to repeat the steps for the variance.

Comparison of sample and theoretical variance of the distribution

varianceSample <- var(meanExp)
varianceSample
## [1] 0.6323969
varianceTheoretical <- (1 /  * 1 / sqrt(n)) ^ 2
varianceTheoretical
## [1] 0.625

The sample standard deviation is 0.8, while the theoretical is 0.79. The difference between the two is 0.01.

Check if distribution tends to a normal

If theory checks out, the distribution should tend to a normal distribution with mean and standard deviation equal to the sample ones.

xfit <- seq(min(meanExp), max(meanExp), length = 2*n)
yfit <- dnorm(xfit, mean = meanSample, sd = sqrt(varianceSample))

The code above constructs a normal fit from the exponential means. Plot 2 shows such similarity.

  • A note about xfit: the two distributions resemble each other only if there are enough points. In this case I chose to pick at least twice as much points as there are data.
mean(meanSample) + c(-1, 1) * qnorm(.975) * sqrt(varianceSample)/sqrt(n)
## [1] 4.694712 5.187594

The code above calculates the 95% C.I. for the sample mean.

  • In terms of hypothesis this means that \[H_0: -_s = 0\] cannot be rejected.

Furthermore, the Q-Q plot in plot 3 shows that normality is generally speaking a good approximation; this was expected from the CLT, of course, but how do we know we are not dealing with an actual normal distribution? Performing a normality check. In this case I will use Kolmogorov-Smirnov test.

ks.test(xfit, meanExp)
## 
##  Two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test
## 
## data:  xfit and meanExp
## D = 0.202, p-value = 0.004739
## alternative hypothesis: two-sided

Since \(p_{ks} < \alpha\) we reject \[H_0: X \sim {\sf N}(\mu_s, \sigma^2/n)\] and conclude that the two distributions are not, in fact, equally distributed.
